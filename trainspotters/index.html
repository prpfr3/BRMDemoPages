Fanny Johnson - the first Trainspotter

From the May 1935 edition of the Great Western Railway Magazine

The hobby of observing and recording names of engines is one that is widely practiced, and nowhere do its devotees display more enthusiasm than at towns and villages adjacent to the Great Western Railway. Many of our readers will have engaged in this fascinating past-time in their boyhood days.

In their “boyhood” days, we say, for one has always presumed this to be an enthusiasm of the male sex, whether old or young: but recently a simple little pocket-book, home-made by its owner 74 years ago, has come to the notice of the Magazine, and this indicates that one girl at least in the early days of railways found an absorbing interest in watching the engines of the Great Western Railway and recording their names.

Fanny was 14 years old when she started spotting. The cover of her 1861 spotting book lists the pages that the engine names appeared under. Where the top page of numbers refers to 'Narrow Gauge' it actually refers to today's standard gauge which was only used on the GWR from October 1861

Shed Passes

Groups or individuals could apply for permits to visit engine sheds. The one on display dates from 2 June 1922 when was Mr Maskelyne was given permission to visit Tunbridge Wells and Brighton's sheds.

Eastleigh Works Open Day

Once a year during, during the 1960s, usually during the school holidays, Eastleigh Works held Open Days. Recently overhauled locomotives were on display.

This picture, taken in 1960, shows Bulleid Pacific Battle of Britain class 34085 named 501 Squadron receiving the attention of young schoolchildren.

The locomotive had just left the works having been rebuilt from its original air-smoothed design.

Tales of a Trainspotter 1962 - Aged 15

Living in Mitcham at the time, my day would start by taking the number 220 bus to Scrubs Lane where it crossed the Grand Union Canal. A short walk along the canal tow path brought me to the broken-down fence at the back of Old Oak Common engine shed. Old Oak Common, the main shed for Paddington, was easy to bunk. I visited dozens of times and was never thrown out. Willesden, a short walk away. Again, an easy shed to visit with the chance of a few cops. Being the main shed for Euston, something from the north might be on shed.

Next on the list, Camden just outside Euston, always very difficult to bunk. Wait for the foreman’s phone to ring and if he turned to look at the board it might just be possible to get by. Sometimes a detour to Devon’s Road, Bow could be added to the trip, easy to bunk but usually nothing much there.

Visits to Euston, Kings Cross and St Pancras stations ended the day. Looking at the information boards to see when the next express would arrive. Then home on the tube to Balham, electric train to Streatham Common and bus. Just time to update my Ian Allan combined.

Ian Allan (1922-2015)

The name Ian Allan is synonymous with trainspotting. He was born in 1922 and on leaving school worked in the Southern Railway Public Relations Office. There he received many requests from the public about the engines and rolling stock of the Southern Railway. In December 1942 he produced a notebook which was well received. With the, eventual, support of the Southern Railway management he went on to produce books for other companies, he called them ABCs.

In 1945 Ian Allan resigned from the Railway to form his own publishing company, which after a few moves had its offices by Shepperton Railway Station. The company went on to produce many 1000s of transport books and magazines.

In 1946 Ian Allan founded Trains Illustrated magazine. The first 8 issues appeared irregularly during 1946 and 1947 due to problems with paper supply post-war. From February 1950 it was a monthly publication, and it was renamed Modern Railways in 1962.

The photo shows Ian driving on the Great Cockcrow Railway at Lyne near Chertsey which he bought in 1960. This 7¼ inch gauge railway operates on Sundays between May and October.

Ian Allan Locospotters' Club

With his wife he formed the Ian Allan Locospotters' Club in 1949, which eventually grew to some 230,000 members.

Three types of Locospotters Club badge were available for purchase; plastic, tin and metal. All were produced in six colours resembling the six British Railways regions:-

  • Green for Southern,
  • Brown for Western
  • Maroon for Midland
  • Dark Blue for Eastern
  • Light Blue for Scotland
  • Orange for North Eastern

Locospotters' Specials

The Ian Allan Locospotters Club organised special trips to places of interest. This trip, on Wednesday the 12th April 1950, during the Easter holiday, to the Isle of Wight included special trains to tour the Island. The trip would have been able to travel over all the railways as the first closure was of the Ventnor West branch in 1952. The over 16 fare of 18/- would be £37 today.

Wednesday 5th April 1961, during the Easter holiday. Ian Allan Locospotters Club Special to Ashford Works and then on to Eastleigh Works. 30117 and 31019 are seen passing Waterloo East having left Charing Cross at 8.51am. At Ashford the spotters had 2 hours to collect the numbers and 1 hour 10 minutes at Eastleigh. The train returned to Waterloo at 6.36pm.

Lyons Maid Trainspotting Train Cards

In 1962 Lyons Maid produced a set of 50 Trainspotting cards. An album was available to hold the cards. This set plus many others made by Lyons Maid were known as “Lolly Cards” beginning distributed packed in lollies.

Francis Bourgeois

Francis Bourgeois is the name Luke Nicholson uses in his social media activity on Instagram and TikTok. Born on July 8th, 2000 in London he became popular during the COVID-19 pandemic for his series of videos posted on TikTok showing him trainspotting.

He has 69.7M likes and 3.1M followers on TikTok. He produces numerous videos on YouTube.
